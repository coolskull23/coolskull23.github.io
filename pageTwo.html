Clean energy sources

Hydrogen fuel cells and other clean initiatives

What Are Hydrogen Fuel Cells:

Hydrogen fuel cells are devices that convert chemical energy through an electrochemical reaction such as that contained in hydrogen molecules into electrical energy since it is a purely clean process, one involving gas hydrogen and oxygen. In this electrochemical reaction, the function of fuel is done by the gas hydrogen while the oxidizing agent (oxygen) is usually supplied from the air. The only byproducts are water and heat, making fuel cells an environmentally friendly alternative to fossil fuels. They are used in different applications including transport, stationary power, and portable power devices offering green energy solution that has the potentiality of minimizing greenhouse gases.

Practicality:

Hydrogen fuel cells are increasingly becoming practical in many domains, especially in the transport applications because they promote vehicles such as a car and a powerful bus that has an efficiency ratio than any other engines and that have minimal waste-emission. They also play key roles in the supply of stationary power hence ensuring a reliable source of clean energy to homes, businesses, and industries. They are also very useful in emergency power supplies as well as the remote use applications and portable electronics since it is possible to carry them to such sites or situations unlike conventional sources where no prior installation of the same has occurred.

Cost:

Although hydrogen fuel cells are very much advertised for their green virtues, their costs remain high due to the expense of the platinum in their catalysts, not to mention the difficulties in manufacturing and storing hydrogen. Today, the fuel cell system for a vehicle application like fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEVs) costs between $50 to $100 per kilowatt. For stationary applications like residential power, it is between $3,000 to $4,500 per kilowatt. Nevertheless, costs will come down with further research, technological improvements, and economies of scale. Economic considerations will soon have it that the fuel cells will improve competitiveness and sustainability, as more companies and manufacturers will produce renewable hydrogen and improve the processes.

Other Initiatives:

Hydrogen fuel cells are a great source of clean energy however, it is important that we use other clean energy sources, as each one comes with strengths and weaknesses.

Solar Power:

The technologies include photovoltaic (PV) panels and solar thermal systems. PV panels contain semiconductor material (such as silicon) in a fashion such that the photovoltaic effect directly changes sunlight into electricity. The sunlight hitting the PV cells also excites the electrons that results in the flow of an electrical current. Concentrating the sunlight by the use of mirrors or lenses onto a receiver, the heated fluid is then circulated to be used to generate steam and drive a turbine in order to produce electricity.

  • Tax Rebates and Benefits: Solar energy projects can and often do receive a variety of government-based incentives, including tax rebates and credits, to help them along. For instance, in the US, the Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC) allows residential and commercial consumers to subtract 30% from their taxesᅳwhen it comes time to file their returnsᅳof the cost of their solar installations; a sizeable reduction overall in the amount of investment required. These incentives are not only making solar power more affordable, but also accelerating the payback for the investments made on solar energy systems.
  • Practicality: It applies on any scale, from the smallest residential setup to huge, multi-megawatt solar farms. They can be grid-connected or deployed in off-grid scenariosᅳespecially in remote and rural areasᅳwhere traditional power infrastructure is non-feasible. The practical nature of solar power can be seen with its low maintenance and solar panels often having long lifetimes exceeding 25 years.
  • Setup and Cost: The initial setup cost for solar power systems can vary widely depending on the scale and specific technology used. Home systems would range from $15,000 to $25,000 before incentives, and tax rebates could mitigate this, so it comes down to the ways people would save money off their electric bill. It comes complete with a site assessment, system design and installation that can be completed within just a few days for residential systems. The falling cost of solar panels and better efficiency have greatly improved the economic viability of solar energy.

    • Wind Energy:

    Wind energy is captured using wind turbines, which convert the kinetic energy of wind into mechanical power. The blades are so designed in a wind turbine to thereby harness the energy in the wind and drive it round in a circle moving a rotor connected to a main shaft, which drives a generator for generating electricity. The amount of power generated depends on the wind speed, air density, and the size of the turbine's rotor.

  • Tax Rebates and Benefits: Like solar, many wind energy projects will include some government incentives, such as tax credits or rebates. In most regions, the production tax credits (PTC) give a per-kilowatt-hour tax credit for electricity generated by eligible wind turbines during the initial years of operation. These incentives lower the cost barrier and stimulate investment in wind energy projects.
  • Practicality: Wind turbines can be placed in many different settings, both offshore and onshore. In such locations, large wind farms typically cluster several turbines in such sites, but where space is at a premium, smaller turbines often serve individual businesses or rural applications. Practicality of application related to the use of wind energy is really achieved with its scale and the global distribution of wind resources, which makes it really available in various regions.
  • Setup and Cost: The cost of installing a wind turbine varies based on the turbine's size and the project's complexity. Onshore wind farms have an average total capital cost, per installed kilowatt, that generally varies from $1,200 to $2,800, whereas offshore wind farms can typically cost more on account of the higher degree of marine installation engineering. In this regard, prices for electricity generated by wind have fallen to record lows, at levels where the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) from wind is in many locations, by far, the most competitive source for new power.
